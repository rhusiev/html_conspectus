# Reflections
## First interview
During my first meeting with Aaron, we had the opportunity to introduce ourselves and discuss our mutual interests and life experiences. As the conversation continued, I shared with him that I had lived in Georgia for five years before returning to Ukraine at the age of six. So we decided to talk about the differences between Georgia and Ukraine in the first interview, which I initially thought would be quite formal and objective.

However, to my surprise, the conversation turned out to be more personal, as Aaron was more interested in my experience and thoughts as an individual. He asked thought-provoking questions that required me to reflect on my beliefs and interests, rather than simply providing him with factual information that he could easily find online. Additionally, only part of the questions were connected to Georgia, as Aaron asked about different topics, such as differences between parts of Ukraine, my attitude towards people from the Eastern part and some life-influencing decisions.

Overall, my first meeting with Aaron was a positive experience. Although I found it challenging at times to express myself in the way I wanted, I appreciated the opportunity to engage in such a meaningful conversation. Not only did it help Aaron with his task, but also gave me an opportunity to reflect on myself and recall some old memories.

## Second interview
When preparing for the second interview, I genuinely did not know what to ask. The United States of America is a country that is well-known globally, primarily through movies, online content, mass media, and popular culture. However, this interaction is mediated by other people with already established views on the world and what should be told in a particular situation. Therefore, I wanted to take another look at the country and culture: from the inside, how it feels to live there and how a regular person sees it.

To achieve this, I asked some questions about people's interests, beliefs, views on the world, and how they live their lives. I was curious to know how Americans perceive their country and culture, and what their daily lives look like. I wanted to understand their attitudes towards different aspects of life, such as politics, religion, and education. I also was curious how the war in Ukraine is perceived there now.

Interestingly, it became more difficult to convey the meaning of questions than to answer them. Perhaps it was due to poor connection or being tired, but it was more common than during the first interview to repeat or paraphrase the sentences when I was asking.

The meeting was quite interesting though. It was not revolutionary in any way, but I appreciated the opportunity to see the picture as it is.

## Differently
After reviewing our initial meeting, I found that while it was generally a positive experience, there were some areas that could benefit from improvement. One aspect that stood out to me was my consistent gaze out of the window, rather than direct eye contact with the camera. While it is easier for me to avoid contact, as the emotions of the other person can be distracting, I realize now that I should have made a greater effort to maintain it.

Additionally, I had prepared a lot of information for our discussion by asking my mother about the experiences in Georgia, including differences between cultures and the impact of moving between countries. However, I was disappointed to find that only a small number of these questions were addressed during the interview, despite the time I had dedicated to preparation. Despite this, I still found the insights gained through my research valuable, and believe that they will be useful in the future.

When preparing for our second meeting, I struggled to come up with meaningful questions to ask. Western culture, including American, is well-known globally, and it can be challenging to find something new. I attempted to focus on personal observations, but the interview seemed to conclude rather quickly.

After the fact, I thought of several more topics of discussion, such as American attitudes towards China, TikTok, and security in general. From what I had heard, Americans tend to take security matters more seriously than their European counterparts. Unfortunately, it was too late.

Although there were some minor challenges, I still appreciate the opportunity to engage in a discussion with someone from a different country and to ask questions in return. I found the conversation interesting, and while it may not have been revolutionary, It was great for self-reflection and understanding of another culture.

## Two things I learned
Unfortunately, I probably gained nothing special by interviewing Aaron,  as most of what he shared was already familiar to me and he just filled in some details. Interestingly, I found the preparations for the first conversations to be more fruitful in this regard, because our family moved from Georgia to Ukraine when I was six, so I did not remember a lot and could explore it.

For example, I did not know about the attitude to the children. It turns out that Georgians adore them. It even happens sometimes that kids are carried on hands even when they are seven years old. As a result, my family became friends with a lot of people and me and my brother were links with others. However, this also leads to children getting everything they want, which is rather a negative side of the culture, as it can easily spoil them.

Another interesting consequence of child-centric culture is huge respect towards a mother. For a man, mom is even of more value than his wife. It becomes even more intriguing, considering the fact that women are generally treated worse and have less rights in Eastern countries, including Georgia. For instance, they usually eat separately from men during celebrations, being considered as servants for the main event.

The interview also made me reflect on my own identity. When asked about the differences between the Western and Eastern parts of Ukraine, I managed to tell them, but then felt that I could not fully associate myself with any. This is partly due to my upbringing, which was quite unusual. I was born in Lviv, but spent the first year in Austria in an international university and my first language was English, which I forgot, though, when we moved to Georgia. I was exposed to various languages, cultures, races and religions there. This experience made me feel less attached to a particular county or mindset, but rather opened my mind to the others.

When I was six, we moved to Ukraine, so now I feel Ukrainian. However I do not see myself as a representation of traditions and worldviews. I am a product of cultural mixing, which has shaped my identity in unique ways. I had not looked at myself like that before. This project, though, helped me see some thing from a different perspective.

Initially, I thought I would not gain anything useful out of this. I was the one primarily interviewed and no questions cropped up for the second meeting. However, interestingly enough, the most fruitful part was not during the conversations, but in-between, through reflections.
